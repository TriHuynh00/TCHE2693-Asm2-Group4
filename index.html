Important Foundational Concepts

Constructive Alignment

A teaching approach focused on student outcomes where intended learning outcomes (ILOs) are defined before teaching begins, with teaching and assessment methods designed to achieve those outcomes.

Two core principles of constructive alignment are:

Constructive: Based on constructivism - learners build knowledge through their own activities.

Alignment: Teaching methods and assessment tasks aligned with intended learning outcomes

A constructively aligned course can be designed in 4 steps

Use one or two verbs to define what students should achieve at the end of the course.

Design teaching and learning activities (TLAs) that engage students with the ILO verbs.

Create assessments using the same verbs to judge performance against rubrics.

Convert assessment judgments into final grades.

Bloom Taxonomy in Constructive Alignment

Using Bloom's hierarchy of verbs to define Intended Learning Outcomes and align teaching activities and assessments. For example:

  • Intended Learning Outcomes: Solve a disciplinary problem by applying expectancy-value theory.
  • Teaching Activity: Case study requiring application of theory to solve a classroom problem
  • Assessment: Evaluation of how well students solved the problem using rubrics

Course Design Example

Design an Annotated Bibliography Workshop

This sample workshop aims to guide students in creating annotated bibliography, a summary of goals, methods, limitations, key findings, and critical evaluation on the relevance of a citation to their research.

Through two individual assessments and hands-on or asynchronous activities, students will learn how to effectively analyze, select, and summarize academic sources relevant to their research using annotated bibliography tools applied by researchers in the field.

Explore the course elements by clicking the items below.

Intended Learning Outcomes (ILOs)

ILO1

Identify and select reliable, relevant resources with respect to the research interests from various academic and credible sources, e.g., research database and libraries, journals, conferences. Â 

ILO2

Understand and apply RMIT-recognized citation rules to reference selected sources. For examples: APA, RMIT Harvard, IEEE.

ILO3

Critically evaluate and concisely summarize the goals, methods, scope, limitations, usefulness, and relevance of each selected source based on its authors, publications, findings and target audience.

ILO4

Apply digital tools (e.g., Zotero, Mendely) to effectively organize and annotate selected sources.

Assessments

Assessment 1 - Individual (40%)

Research Question Development & Source Evaluation Essay

This assessment is designed to help students develop ability to construct a research question, find credible academic sources to answer the research question, and critically evaluate the sources. Students are required to write an academic essay to address the following requirements:

Construct a specific research question from one of three topics (climate change, social media and mental health, or AI in education).

Select 3 - 4 reliable and relevant academic sources (journal articles, conference papers, or book chapters) related to your research topic.

For each source, summarize the purpose and scope of the work, methodology, findings, and evaluate the relevance of the source to help you answer the research question.

Provide full citations for all sources using the RMIT-Harvard citation style, both in-text and in the reference list.
